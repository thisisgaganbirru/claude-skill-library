# Content Scripting Framework — Full Reference

## The Philosophy of Script Writing for Creators

A script is not a teleprompter. It is a **thinking tool** — a pre-flight checklist that ensures every second of content earns its place. The best creators don't sound scripted because they've internalized the script's structure so deeply that it becomes instinct. Your job when scripting for a creator is to give them a skeleton that is tight enough to keep them on track but loose enough that their personality fills every gap.

The golden rule: **a script should read like the creator thinks, not like a document someone wrote.** If the creator talks in slang, the script has slang. If they run-on their sentences for emphasis, the script does too. If they make pop culture references, the script earns those references.

Scripting is not one size fits all. It varies across three axes:

**Platform** — A TikTok script is ruthlessly compressed; a YouTube long-form script is architecturally complex; a podcast script is conversational scaffolding. The words themselves, the pacing notes, even the punctuation style all change based on where this content lives.

**Content type** — A tutorial script is structured around steps. A story-based script is structured around tension and payoff. A hot-take script is structured around a provocation and evidence. Each has its own internal logic.

**Creator voice** — This is the most important axis. Two creators making the same video about the same topic for the same platform should have completely different scripts. Voice calibration is covered in depth in its own section below.

## Part 1: Voice Calibration — Writing in Someone Else's Voice

Before writing a single word of a script, calibrate the creator's voice. This is how the skill learns to write *as them*, not *about their topic*.

### The 5-Dimension Voice Profile

To write in a creator's voice, assess them across five dimensions. Ask the user to share existing content, describe themselves, or answer these questions directly.

**1. Energy Level**
How does this creator show up energetically? Options range from: calm and measured (think: Ali Abdaal, Therapy-style creators), to warm and conversational (think: educational but not clinical), to high-energy and punchy (think: typical fitness/motivation content), to chaotic-fun (think: comedy or Gen Z content where humor is the vehicle).

This determines sentence length, punctuation rhythm, whether lines end in full stops or trail off with ellipses, and how much exclamation vs. deadpan delivery gets written in.

**2. Vocabulary Range**
Does this creator use industry-specific terms or do they consciously break everything down into plain English? Do they use Gen Z slang natively (e.g., "lowkey", "ngl", "no cap", "slay", "it's giving") or would that feel forced for them? Do they code-switch (casual with their audience, professional when citing data)?

Write at the exact vocabulary level the creator actually uses. Writing above it makes them sound fake; writing below it makes them sound dumb.

**3. Personal Reference Style**
Does this creator reference their own life heavily (personal stories as the primary vehicle for all points) or do they stay more idea-focused (the content is the star, they're the narrator)? Some creators say "I was at the gym last Tuesday and I realized..." for everything; others never bring themselves into it at all. Neither is wrong — but the script has to match.

**4. Humor Style**
Does this creator use humor, and if so, what kind? Self-deprecating (audiences love this), absurdist (harder to pull off but extremely viral when it lands), dry wit (think: subtle, usually one-liner style), or no humor at all (clinical, authoritative tone)? Forcing humor into a creator's script when they're not a "funny" creator will make them look unnatural on camera.

**5. Sentence Rhythm**
Read their existing content aloud and listen for the rhythm. Some creators talk in short punchy bursts. Boom. Like this. Period after everything. Others talk in longer, more flowing sentences that connect one idea to the next in a single breath before landing the point. Some use repetition for emphasis ("This is the key. This is what nobody tells you. This."). Replicate the rhythm in the script.

### Voice Profile Template

When beginning a scripting task, produce a quick voice profile before writing:

```
VOICE PROFILE: [Creator Name / Handle]
Energy: [calm / warm / high-energy / chaotic]
Vocabulary: [casual Gen Z / plain conversational / professional / technical]
Personal Style: [story-heavy / idea-focused / data-driven / personality-forward]
Humor: [self-deprecating / dry / absurdist / none]
Sentence Rhythm: [short bursts / flowing / repetition-heavy / mixed]
Signature phrases or patterns: [any recurring language, sign-offs, intros, etc.]
```

Always show this to the creator before writing the full script and ask them to correct anything. Getting this wrong means the entire script needs to be rewritten from scratch.

## Part 2: What to Talk About — The Content Planning Layer

Script writing begins before the first word. Choosing the right topic, the right angle, and the right depth is the planning layer that precedes scripting.

### The Topic Selection Framework

Use the following four-filter process to decide what a piece of content should be about:

**Filter 1 — Audience Need (Demand)**
Is there evidence that your target audience actively wants this content? Evidence can come from: search volume data (YouTube autocomplete, Google trends, keyword tools), comment sections on your own or competitors' posts (what are they asking for?), Reddit/Quora threads in the niche, or DM questions you've received. If you can't point to evidence of demand, you're making content you *think* people want — which is how creators stall.

**Filter 2 — Creator Authority (Can You Deliver?)**
Can this creator speak on this topic with genuine experience or expertise? The audience can feel when a creator is talking about something they've actually lived vs. something they Googled to make content. Authority doesn't mean credentials — it means real-world skin in the game. A broke college student talking about "how I saved $500 last month" has more authority on that specific topic than a certified financial planner who's never been broke.

**Filter 3 — Content Pillar Fit**
Does this topic map to one of the creator's established content pillars? If yes, prioritize it — it builds topic authority and keeps the channel coherent. If no, ask whether it's a strategic one-off (trend-jacking a viral moment) or whether it suggests the creator's pillar set needs to evolve.

**Filter 4 — Format Viability**
Is this topic better as a short-form piece (< 60s) or long-form? Can it be adequately covered in 45 seconds, or does it require depth that a longer format earns? Trying to compress a 15-minute topic into a 30-second Reel produces a surface-level piece that satisfies no one. Stretching a 60-second idea into a 10-minute YouTube video produces padded content that tanks retention.

### What To Actually Say — The Angle Selection

The same topic can be covered from dozens of different angles. Angle selection is often more important than topic selection, because it determines whether a piece of content feels new or derivative.

The five most viral content angles (applicable to any topic in any niche):

**1. The Contrarian**
Take the most common piece of advice in your niche and disagree with it, backed by experience or logic. "Everyone says to post every day on Instagram. Here's why that actually killed my account." The contrarian angle works because it violates expectations, which is the first requirement of stopping a scroll.

**2. The Confession / "I Was Wrong"**
Admit a mistake, a misconception, or an embarrassing belief you used to hold. "I spent 2 years trying to grow my YouTube channel and I was doing it completely backwards." Vulnerability + insight = watch time. The confession angle builds parasocial trust faster than almost anything else.

**3. The "What Nobody Tells You"**
Surface the information that exists but isn't being talked about clearly. This angle positions the creator as the person who has done the research so their audience doesn't have to. Works best for informational niches (finance, health, tech, career).

**4. The Personal Story (With Stakes)**
Tell a story where something was genuinely at risk — money, relationships, reputation, health, identity. The story must have a clear before/after and a lesson that the audience can apply. "I quit my $80K job to be a full-time creator. 6 months later, here's what actually happened." The key: don't skip the emotional stakes. The numbers are interesting; the feelings are viral.

**5. The Tutorial With a Twist**
A how-to video is table stakes. A how-to video with an unexpected or counterintuitive approach is discoverable. "How to batch 30 days of content in one afternoon" vs. "How to plan content" — the first has a built-in promise that sounds unbelievable enough to click.

## Part 3: Platform-Specific Script Formats

### TikTok / Instagram Reels Script (15–60 seconds)

The short-form script is the most unforgiving format because there is no room for setup. Every element must justify its existence.

**Structure:**
```
[HOOK — 0 to 1.5 seconds]
One sentence. Spoken or on-screen text. Must create an immediate reason to keep watching.
No intro. No "hey guys". No context. Straight into the hook.

[PROMISE / TEASE — 1.5 to 4 seconds]
One to two sentences. Tell them what they're going to get if they watch the whole thing.
"By the end of this I'll give you the exact three things that changed everything."
This is the micro-commitment that converts passive watchers into active viewers.

[BODY — 4 to 45 seconds depending on length]
Deliver the value. Fast. No filler. Use a numbered list structure ("number one...") if covering multiple points — it creates natural re-hook moments between each point.
Each point should be 1–3 sentences maximum.
If there are more than 3 points, split it into a series.

[LOOP / CTA — final 2–3 seconds]
Either: a soft CTA ("save this for later", "follow for more [specific thing]")
Or: a loop hook — a line that makes the viewer want to rewatch or continue (great for building completion rate).
Avoid hard sells in short-form CTAs. "Follow me for more" is weak. "Follow me if you're the type of person who [specific identity]" is much stronger.
```

**Script length:** A 30-second script is approximately 75–90 words spoken at a natural but energetic pace. A 60-second script is approximately 150–180 words.

**Pacing notes to include in the script:**
Mark where on-screen text appears. Mark where cuts should happen (every 2–4 seconds on TikTok is optimal). If the creator is pointing at things or reacting, note that in brackets. Example: `[cut to close-up here]` or `[text overlay: "THIS is what kills growth"]`.

**Example short-form script (fitness niche, 30 seconds, high-energy voice):**

```
[HOOK — say directly to camera, no intro]
"The reason your abs aren't showing has nothing to do with your workout."

[PROMISE]
"It's this — and it's something 90% of people at the gym are getting completely wrong."

[BODY]
"Your abs are already there. What's covering them is inflammation from processed food — not fat.
Swap out ultra-processed snacks for whole foods for just two weeks.
The difference will actually shock you."
[text overlay: "it's not the crunches"]

[CTA]
"Save this. You'll want it when you think it's not working."
```

### YouTube Long-Form Script (7–20 minutes)

Long-form YouTube is a completely different animal. The audience has opted into a longer time investment, which means the contract with them is different — they came for depth, and you owe them depth. The script is architecturally complex, with multiple re-hook moments designed to prevent drop-off.

**Structural Blueprint:**

```
[FALSE OPEN — 0 to 30 seconds]
Start in the middle of the story or at the most compelling moment.
Do NOT start with "hey welcome back to my channel" or a logo intro.
Drop the audience directly into a scene, a claim, or a moment.
Example: "Three months ago I had $0 in savings. Today I'm going to show you exactly how I changed that."
The false open delays the intro but creates immediate emotional investment.

[INTRO — 30 to 60 seconds]
Now you can introduce yourself, briefly, and set up what the video will cover.
Keep it to 3–4 sentences. Tease the specific payoff they'll get by watching to the end.
This is also where the SEO keyword should appear in your spoken words.

[CONTEXT / SETUP — 1 to 3 minutes]
Establish why this topic matters. Build the stakes.
This section does most of the emotional work — it gets the viewer to care before you start delivering value.
Common approaches: share a personal backstory, cite a surprising statistic, present the problem the audience is experiencing.

[VALUE BODY — the bulk of the video]
This is where you deliver on the promise. Structure it around 3–5 main points or steps.
Each main point follows this mini-structure:
  - State the point (one clear sentence)
  - Explain why it matters or why most people get it wrong
  - Give a specific example, story, or demonstration
  - Transition to the next point with a bridge line ("which brings me to the part most people skip entirely...")
The bridge lines are crucial — they function as re-hooks that reset the viewer's attention at each transition.

[PATTERN INTERRUPTS throughout]
Every 60–90 seconds, introduce a pattern interrupt to reset attention:
  - Cut to B-roll or a different camera angle
  - Introduce a graphic, chart, or text overlay
  - Ask a rhetorical question directly to the viewer
  - Share a quick anecdote or relatable moment before returning to the main content

[PRE-CONCLUSION TEASE — 30 seconds before the end]
Before wrapping up, tease one final insight, bonus tip, or "the most important thing I haven't told you yet."
This rewards viewers who watched all the way through and dramatically increases watch time on the final section.

[CONCLUSION + CTA — final 1–2 minutes]
Summarize the 3 key takeaways in 3 sentences or less.
Give a clear, single CTA — don't stack multiple asks ("like, comment, subscribe, follow my Instagram, buy my course...").
Pick ONE: either subscribe, or leave a comment answering a specific question, or check out a related video.
End with a personal sign-off that fits the creator's voice.
```

**Script length for long-form:** 7 minutes ≈ 1,000–1,200 words. 15 minutes ≈ 2,000–2,500 words. Speaking pace for YouTube is generally 130–150 words per minute for educational content; faster for entertainment.

**Retention engineering notes to include in the script:**
At each major transition point, add a note like `[RETENTION CHECK: re-hook needed here]` to remind the creator to deliver the bridge line with energy. Mark the false open clearly. Mark the pre-conclusion tease so it doesn't accidentally get cut in editing.

### Instagram Carousel Script (Static / Swipe Post)

A carousel is a visual story told across 5–10 slides. It's the highest-save format on Instagram, which makes it the most algorithm-friendly format for growing reach with existing followers and attracting new ones through Shares.

**Structure:**
```
[SLIDE 1 — The Hook Slide]
This is your thumbnail. It must do two jobs: stop the scroll AND create a reason to swipe.
Big, bold headline. 5–10 words maximum. Often phrased as a promise, a question, or a bold statement.
No body text on slide 1. Just the hook.
Example: "5 things every creator gets wrong in their first year"

[SLIDES 2–8 — The Value Slides]
Each slide delivers one idea, one tip, one step, or one data point.
The slide title is the point. The body text (2–4 lines max) is the explanation.
Maintain visual consistency — same font, same color palette, same layout grid.
Include a subtle "swipe →" cue on slides 2–7 to keep the reader moving.

[SLIDE 9 (or second-to-last) — The Revelation / Best Tip]
Save your strongest insight for near the end. This rewards people who made it through and
gives them something worth screenshotting or saving — which is the behavior you want.
Label it: "The one most people skip:" or "Here's the real answer:"

[FINAL SLIDE — The CTA Slide]
One clear action. Either: "Save this for later" (drives the save signal) or
"Share this with a creator who needs to hear it" (drives shares) or
"Follow for more [specific thing]" (drives follows from engaged readers).
Add your handle prominently on this slide for when it gets shared without context.
```

**Caption for the carousel:**
The caption does NOT need to repeat the slides. Instead, write a caption that adds a personal layer — a quick story about why you made this, your own experience with the topic, or a question to the audience. This turns a purely educational carousel into a relationship-building post.

### Podcast / Long-Form Talking-Head Script (YouTube, Spotify, etc.)

Podcasts and talking-head style videos are the most conversational format. Fully word-for-word scripts usually sound too stiff for this format. Instead, use a **structured outline with talking points** rather than a verbatim script.

**Structure:**
```
[COLD OPEN — the most interesting/entertaining moment from the conversation]
In solo podcasts: a provocative question or personal story that drops mid-scene.
In interview formats: pull the most quotable moment and tease it at the top ("later in this conversation, X said something that completely changed how I think about Y...")

[INTRO (scripted) — 60–90 seconds]
This section should be fully scripted because it contains the episode title, guest intro, and SEO keywords.
State the episode topic, who is being featured (if applicable), and what the listener will walk away with.

[TOPIC BLOCKS — loosely scripted]
Break the episode into 3–5 major topic blocks.
For each block, write: the opening question or statement to kick off the topic, 3–5 bullet points of what should be covered, and a transition line to move to the next block.
Do NOT fully script these sections — let the conversation breathe. The bullet points are guardrails, not a script.

[SPONSOR SEGMENT — fully scripted if applicable]
Sponsor reads perform better when they're story-based ("I actually use this product and here's when it came in useful...") rather than straight reads. Write the sponsor copy in the creator's voice.

[CLOSE — scripted]
Summarize 2–3 takeaways. Give the CTA (subscribe, review, join the newsletter, follow the guest). Sign off in the creator's signature style.
```

## Part 4: The Script Review Checklist

Before delivering any script to a creator, run through this checklist:

**Hook strength**: Read the first line out loud. Does it create an immediate, specific reason to keep watching? If you could imagine it being the first line of a viral video, proceed. If it sounds like an intro, rewrite it.

**Filler audit**: Search the script for: "um", "so basically", "as I was saying", "you know what I mean", "at the end of the day", and "in conclusion." Remove all of them unless they are intentional stylistic choices for the creator's voice.

**Pacing check**: Read the script aloud with a timer. Is it the right length for the platform? A 30-second TikTok script should take 28–32 seconds to read at speaking pace — not 45 seconds.

**Voice consistency**: Does every sentence sound like the creator, or are there moments where the writing slips into formal/generic language? Flag any sentence that a creator would feel weird saying and rewrite it.

**CTA clarity**: Is there one CTA and is it specific? "Like and subscribe" is weak. "Comment below: which of these 3 strategies are you trying first?" is specific, conversation-starting, and gets people in the comments — which is the high-value signal.

**SEO keyword check**: For YouTube scripts — was the primary keyword spoken naturally in the first 60 seconds? For TikTok — was the topic keyword spoken in the first 10 seconds?

## Part 5: Script + Content Plan Delivery Format

When delivering a scripted piece of content, always include the full package:

```
CONTENT PLAN
─────────────────────────────
Platform: [where this posts]
Format: [Reel / YouTube long-form / Carousel / etc.]
Content Pillar: [which of the creator's pillars this maps to]
Target Keyword: [primary SEO keyword if applicable]
Hook Strategy: [which hook type was used and why]
Why This Topic Now: [the demand signal or timing reason]
Expected Outcome: [what metric this piece is designed to move — reach, saves, comments, subscribers]

SCRIPT
─────────────────────────────
[Full script in the creator's voice, with platform-specific structural notes in brackets]

CAPTION
─────────────────────────────
[Platform-appropriate caption copy, with hashtags if applicable]

A/B HOOK VARIATIONS
─────────────────────────────
[2 alternative opening lines the creator can test against the primary hook]

THUMBNAIL / COVER DIRECTION (if applicable)
─────────────────────────────
[Brief description of what the thumbnail or carousel cover image should communicate and look like]
```

This full-package format means the creator receives everything they need to go from idea to published in one sitting — no gaps that require them to figure out the caption separately or wonder what the thumbnail should be.
